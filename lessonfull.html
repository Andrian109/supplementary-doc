3.1. Maceration (Cold or Hot Soaking)

Principle: Plant material is soaked in a solvent at room temperature or slightly warmed, allowing compounds to diffuse into the solvent.

Procedure:

  • Grind plant material to increase surface area.
  • Soak in solvent (e.g., 70% EtOH) for hours to days.
  • Stir/agitate to enhance diffusion.
  • Filter and collect the extract.

Advantages:

  • Simple and inexpensive.
  • Preserves heat-sensitive compounds (e.g., anthocyanins).

Limitations:

  • Time-consuming (24–72 hours).
  • May not extract all compounds (less efficient).
  • Safety: Use gloves and work in a fume hood when handling volatile solvents like methanol.

Figure: maceration

3.2. Soxhlet Extraction

Principle: Continuous cycling of hot solvent through plant material for efficient extraction.

Procedure:

  • Place plant powder in a thimble.
  • Heat solvent; it evaporates, condenses, and drips over the sample repeatedly.
  • Cycle for 6–24 hours.
  • Collect the extract.

Advantages:

  • More efficient than maceration.
  • Minimal solvent loss.

Limitations:

  • Unsuitable for heat-sensitive compounds (e.g., some phenolics).
  • Requires specialized equipment.
  • Safety: Ensure proper ventilation; handle hot solvents with care to avoid burns.

Figure: Soxhlet extraction

3.3. Microwave-Assisted Extraction (MAE)

Principle: Microwaves rapidly heat solvents and plant tissue, rupturing cell walls to release compounds. Aligns with green chemistry by reducing solvent use.

Procedure:

  • Mix plant material with solvent in a closed microwave-safe vessel.
  • Apply microwaves for 1–10 minutes.
  • Filter and collect the extract.

Advantages:

  • Fast (minutes vs. hours).
  • High efficiency, low solvent use.

Limitations:

  • Expensive equipment.
  • Requires optimization to prevent degradation (e.g., anthocyanins in Curcuma longa).
  • Safety: Use microwave-safe vessels; avoid overheating to prevent explosions.
